A Note on Scope

Why this isn't a "case study"

Unlike Durian FHC, where I owned media planning end-to-end, Zepto was a different kind of role. I didn't set the strategy or own client-facing decisions. My work was execution and reporting: turning the planned campaign structure into reality every single day, across three categories, at a scale where a missed step means lakhs of rupees going the wrong way overnight.

I wanted this page to be honest about that distinction. What follows isn't about strategy. It's about what it actually took to keep this machine running."

The Three Categories

Three engines, three different KPIs, running every day

Zepto's UA account wasn't one campaign. It was three distinct operations, each with its own logic, audience, and success metric.

1. Core UAC: Established Cities
Spend: ₹75–125Cr/month at peak KPI: First Orders CAC: ₹130–180
Running across Bangalore, Delhi, Kolkata, Pune, Mumbai, Chennai, Hyderabad and more — split by Android/iOS and by campaign type (tROAS, TCPA). This was the highest-spend, highest-stakes category, where daily pacing checks and budget reallocation between cities happened constantly.
2. New City / State Launch
Spend: ₹9–17Cr/month KPI: Installs → First Orders Phases: Pre-Launch → Launch → Post-Launch
Every new city got its own campaign lifecycle, independent of the core account. A city wasn't just "added". It went through a Pre-Launch awareness phase, a Launch phase optimised for installs, then a Post-Launch shift toward First Orders, each phase with its own creative set and targeting logic.
3. Remarketing (RMKT)
Spend: ₹20–24Cr/month KPI: Repeat Orders Cost/RO: ₹15–25
Built entirely around recency and frequency cohorts rather than geography. Audience cohorts: Active, Lapsed, ELC (Existing/Lapsed Cohort), and Super Saver segments, each further sliced by windows like "ordered 5+ times in 30 days" or "dormant for 7+ days." A completely different targeting logic from the acquisition side.

Across these three categories, peak combined monthly spend regularly crossed ₹150Cr.

The Daily Reality

What execution at this scale actually looked like

Some things that came up constantly but never show up in any dashboard:

  • Hitting Google's ad limits. At this campaign volume, we regularly hit Google's per-ad-group and per-campaign ad limits. The fix wasn't glamorous. It meant going in and deleting underperforming ads and ad groups to free up space for new product- or category-based creatives, every time we wanted to test something new.
  • Daily pacing across three categories simultaneously. A budget issue in core UAC, a new city hitting its Launch phase, and an RMKT audience refresh could all need attention on the same day. There was no "one thing at a time."
  • Reporting that had to be airtight. With this much spend moving daily, the automated reporting stack (performance, creative, ad strength, post-campaign) wasn't a nice-to-have. It was the only way to catch a problem before it became an expensive one.

This account ran on a different clock. Most days meant 10–12 hours actively in the account, not because of inefficiency, but because the scale and pace genuinely demanded it. The whole team worked this way — the account was growing too fast for it to be any other way.

What I Took From It

Execution discipline at scale

I didn't set strategy here, but I learned what it takes to actually execute someone else's strategy at scale. At a volume where small process gaps compound fast. That discipline is exactly what let me take full ownership on Durian FHC afterward, with the reporting rigor and pacing instincts this account forced me to build first.
